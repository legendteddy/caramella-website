Drawer Runners: Blum and DTC, Explained

When quotes look similar, runner tier is one of the easiest places to hide cost. Here is how to compare it cleanly.

Last updated: 24 February 2026

Quick Answer

Who this page is for: Homeowners comparing kitchen quotes and trying to understand whether a runner upgrade is worth it for feel, alignment, and long-term use.

Use this page to compare runner tiers by model and load rating, not brand name alone.

Why Runner Choice Matters

Drawer runners are one of the most-used mechanical components in a kitchen. A typical kitchen drawer is opened and closed 5–15 times per day — that's 2,000–5,500 cycles per year. Over a 10-year lifespan, runners endure 20,000–55,000 cycles. Poor-quality runners begin to show wear (roughness, misalignment, noise) as early as 5,000 cycles. Premium runners are rated for 80,000+ cycles.

The runner also determines how smoothly and quietly a drawer operates, how much weight it can carry, and whether it self-closes without slamming. In Brunei's humid environment, runner material and plating quality also affect corrosion resistance.

Concealed vs Side-Mounted Runners

There are two main installation types, each with different trade-offs:

  • Concealed undermount: Runner is hidden beneath the drawer box. Clean look — no visible hardware when the drawer is open. Requires precise drawer box dimensions (typically 13mm clearance per side). Most premium kitchens use this type. Both Blum TANDEM and some DTC models offer this.
  • Side-mounted (ball-bearing): Runner is attached to the side of the drawer and cabinet interior. Visible when the drawer is open. Easier to install and more forgiving of dimensional variance. Lower cost. Common in utility drawers, laundry rooms, and budget builds.

Soft-Close: How It Works

Soft-close (or self-close) mechanisms decelerate the drawer in the last 30–50mm of its travel and pull it gently into the fully closed position. This prevents slamming and reduces wear on both the runner and the cabinet.

Quality varies significantly between implementations. Higher-end systems (Blum BLUMOTION) provide consistent, smooth deceleration regardless of how hard the drawer is pushed. Lower-cost soft-close mechanisms may feel inconsistent — working well when the drawer is gently pushed, but failing to fully decelerate when pushed hard. Ask for a demonstration if possible.

Decision Framework

Neither brand is universally "better" — the right choice depends on your priorities:

  • Choose Blum TANDEM if: You prioritise concealed look, consistent soft-close feel, long cycle life, and are willing to pay the premium. Best for main kitchen drawers used daily.
  • Choose DTC if: You need to manage costs across a large project (full house builds), are outfitting utility or secondary areas, or are comparing a specific DTC model that meets your load and cycle requirements. Always verify the specific model's specs rather than relying on the brand name.
  • Consider mixing: Some projects use Blum for primary kitchen drawers (high use) and DTC for secondary areas (bedside drawers, bathroom vanity). This optimises cost without compromising where it matters most.
