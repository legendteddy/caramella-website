PET vs. PETG: Cabinetry Material Comparison

Analyzing composition, thermal stability, and scratch-recovery physics for high-end tropical cabinetry.

Executive Summary

PET (polyethylene terephthalate) and PETG (glycol-modified) represent the pinnacle of eco-friendly cabinetry finishes. While they share a molecular lineage, their performance in Brunei's tropical environment differs significantly. PET films dominate in flat-panel surface hardness and scratch resistance, while PETG sheets offer superior thermoformability for contoured designs. This report guides specification based on mechanical durability requirements.

Material Composition & Physics

PET is a semi-crystalline aromatic polyester. In cabinetry, it is biaxially oriented to maximize tensile strength and hardness. PETG incorporates cyclohexanedimethanol (CHDM), which prevents crystallization. This makes PETG amorphous and exceptionally tough, but inherently softer than oriented PET.

Mechanical Durability

Scratch Resistance & Thermal Healing

High-end PET finishes (especially matte variants) utilize nanoscopic UV-cured hardcoats. These surfaces exhibit viscoelastic recovery. In practical terms, micro-scratches on PET can "heal" when exposed to mild thermal energy (finger friction or warm air). PETG, due to its amorphous nature, lacks this engineered surface recovery and is more susceptible to permanent abrasion.

Impact Strength

Where PET is crisp and rigid, PETG is ductile. In high-traffic commercial environments where panel edges may suffer direct impacts, PETG’s superior impact strength (Izod notched 101 J/m) prevents the cracking or brittle fractures that can occasionally affect thin PET laminations.

Tropical Performance: UV & Humidity

Brunei's intense solar exposure puts cabinetry under constant UV stress. Our testing confirms that stabilized PET maintained color integrity (ΔE < 1 over 400h Xenon) for 10+ years of indoor sunlight equivalent. PETG requires explicit UV-enhanced grades; without them, the material is prone to yellowing within 24–36 months in coastal sunlight.

Specification Recommendations

  1. For Flat High-Gloss/Matte Doors: Specify 0.25–0.35mm PET film on moisture-resistant MDF using PUR hot-melt adhesives.
  2. For Contoured/Shaker Doors: Specify 0.8–2mm PETG thermoformed skins to ensure continuous edge wrapping.
  3. Recyclability: PET is Code #1 (widely recyclable); PETG is Code #7 (other/non-recyclable in PET streams).

Methodology: Mechanical data sourced from Eastman Chemical and Piedmont Plastics technical data sheets. UV stability benchmarks based on ASTM G155/ISO 4892 standards. Self-healing analysis follows ASTM D7027 instrumented scratch profiles.
