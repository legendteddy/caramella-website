PET vs. PETG: Cabinetry Material Comparison

Forget the chemistry lab. Here is the real-world difference between PET and PETG cabinet doors when it comes to fingerprints, fading, and scratch healing.

Last updated:

Quick Answer

Who this page is for: Homeowners looking for that flawless, ultra-matte cabinet look without the stress of permanent scratches.

This breaks down why PET is better for flat, scratch-proof doors, and PETG is better for heavy-impact or curved designs.

Material Standard

This research utilizes ENF-grade (Zero-HCHO) plywood as the baseline for moisture resilience and safety. Highly relevant for RPN and STKRJ housing layouts across Brunei.

Executive Summary

PET and PETG belong to the same eco-friendly plastic family, but they behave very differently in a Bruneian kitchen. PET films are harder, making them incredible at resisting scratches on flat doors. PETG sheets are modified to be softer but much tougher against heavy impacts. Here's how to choose what goes in your house.

The Short Version of the Science

PET is engineered to be as rigid and hard as possible. PETG has an extra molecule added (the "G" stands for Glycol) that stops it from becoming rigid. This makes PETG incredibly tough and bendable, but it also means the surface is naturally softer and easier to scratch than pure PET.

The Spec Breakdown

Feature PET Film (Doors) PETG Panels
Scratch Resistance High (Harder surface) Medium (Softer surface)
Impact Strength (Cracking) Good Exceptional (Won't shatter)
Sunlight (UV) Fading Excellent (Stays true to color) Needs special UV coating

Real-Life Durability

The "Self-Healing" Superpower

High-end PET finishes (especially the trendy ultra-matte ones) have an incredible feature: thermal healing. If you get a micro-scratch from wiping the door or brushing against it, you can literally "heal" the scratch by rubbing it with your finger or using a hairdryer. The heat softens the microscopic surface layer and it flows back together. Because PETG is softer, it can't be engineered with this same self-healing topcoat.

Impact Survival

If you have kids crashing toys into cabinets, or you run a busy commercial kitchen, PETG's softness becomes an advantage. Where a super-hard PET corner might chip if hit hard enough with a heavy pan, PETG will just dent because of its extreme impact strength.

Handling Brunei's Sun and Humidity

Coastal and tropical houses in Brunei get intense sunlight. Pure PET holds its color beautifully without yellowing, even after a decade of morning sun hitting your kitchen island. PETG, however, will turn yellow in 2 to 3 years unless the manufacturer added an expensive UV-protection layer.

Thermal Stability During Raya Prep

Brunei's intense Raya cooking marathons create sustained high-heat environments in wet kitchens. While standard PVC foils can shrink or peel at 60°C, high-grade PET films are thermally stable up to 85°C. When bonded with PUR (Polyurethane) adhesives, which create a chemical cross-link rather than just a physical bond, the doors become virtually immune to the steam and ambient heat of a busy festive kitchen.

Specification Recommendations

  1. For Flat High-Gloss/Matte Doors: Specify 0.25–0.35mm PET film on moisture-resistant MDF using PUR hot-melt adhesives.
  2. For Contoured/Shaker Doors: Specify 0.8–2mm PETG thermoformed skins to ensure continuous edge wrapping.
  3. Recyclability: PET is Code #1 (widely recyclable); PETG is Code #7 (other/non-recyclable in PET streams).

Methodology: Mechanical data sourced from Eastman Chemical and Piedmont Plastics technical data sheets. UV stability benchmarks based on ASTM G155/ISO 4892 standards. Self-healing analysis follows ASTM D7027 instrumented scratch profiles.

Material Selection Advisory

Choosing between PET and PETG depends on your kitchen's sunlight exposure and cooking frequency. Request a site measurement to see physical material samples in your actual lighting conditions.
