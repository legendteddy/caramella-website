# Brunei Climate (Humidity Profiles)  
Brunei has a hot-humid tropical climate with **year-round high relative humidity**.  Band­ar Seri Begawan monthly averages run roughly 81–85% RH【91†L153-L162】【28†L364-L372】, i.e. *“about 80% all year round”*【3†L112-L114】.  In practice RH seldom falls below ~65–70%, and overnight/dawn values routinely approach saturation.  For example, January RH ≈84%, February 83%, … November–December ≈85%【91†L153-L162】.  Sustained RH in the **80–90% range** is the norm in the wet season, often exceeding 90% in the early morning.  (Without active air-conditioning, **indoor RH** in unsealed buildings will track outdoor RH, often 70–90%.)  Thus Brunei environments impose constant high-moisture exposure on materials.  

# Material Hygroscopicity (Plywood vs MDF)  
Wood and wood-panels are highly hygroscopic: they take up moisture from air, swelling as they do so.  An example sorption curve for wood is shown below.  Notably, *small* RH changes (30–60%) yield modest moisture (EMC ~6–11%), but as RH exceeds 85% the bound moisture content (EMC) and swelling climb steeply【60†L113-L121】.  For instance, 50%RH → ~9% EMC, 65%RH → ~12% EMC, and above 85%RH swell rate accelerates【60†L113-L121】.

**Plywood vs MDF:** Both plywood and medium‑density fiberboard (MDF) will absorb moisture, but their behavior differs.  MDF (an engineered fiberboard bonded with urea-formaldehyde or melamine adhesives) is *more uniform* and typically denser (≈600–800 kg/m³) than plywood (often 400–600 kg/m³, cross‑laminated veneers).  MDF’s continuous fiber matrix allows more bound-water absorption per volume.  In practice, **MDF achieves slightly higher EMC** than plywood under the same conditions.  (One source notes MDF’s EMC is slightly *lower* than particleboard but generally comparable【65†L96-L104】; solid wood veneers in plywood yield somewhat lower EMC than MDF【65†L42-L47】.)  Both show pronounced sorption hysteresis (adsorption vs desorption paths differ)【38†L72-L80】.  High-density MDF equilibrates at higher MC than low-density MDF【38†L72-L80】.  In contrast, plywood’s cross‑veneer construction yields near‑zero in-plane expansion (veneers block each other) – expansion occurs mainly through thickness and glue lines.  Thus plywood’s *linear* expansion is minimal, while MDF swells isotropically.

**Equilibrium Moisture Content:**  At ~20°C and 80% RH, typical wood EMC is ~9–10%【60†L113-L121】.  Given MDF’s higher fiber content and hygroscopic UF resin, MDF might reach 10–12% EMC under 80% RH (possibly higher if high humidity persists).  Plywood’s EMC is in a similar range (veener wood, often with phenolic glue) – often cited around 5–8% MC at fabrication, rising toward 9–10% at 80–90% RH.  In summary, both materials will absorb on the order of 10–15% moisture by weight at 80–90% RH.  

**Thickness and Dimension Changes:**  As moisture is absorbed, board dimensions change.  MDF can swell more dramatically (especially in thickness).  Published data show MDF thickness swelling often **exceeds** plywood’s.  For example, MDF thickness can increase several percent under high RH, while cross‑lam plywood typically shows <1–2% thickness swell for the same humidity increment (though specific values depend on grade and adhesives).  Critically, *MDF’s thickness swell from humidity is largely **permanent*** – once MDF swells beyond its original size, it will not fully return on drying【69†L95-L100】.  Plywood tends to recover better (especially if bonded with water-resistant resins), but prolonged saturation or repeated cycles can eventually lead to glue-line creep or delamination.  (See Table below for comparison of key attributes.)  

# Standards, Grades, and Specifications  
- **Plywood:**  Tropical plywood grades (e.g. BS1088, JAS Type 3) use waterproof phenolic or melamine-phenol adhesives.  EN 636 classifies plywood: Class 2 (“humid conditions”) and Class 3 (“exterior”) specify bonding and thickness-swell limits under boiling tests.  Boiling-water-proof (BWP) plywood (marine grade) must survive 72h boiling with <12% thickness swell.  Typical plywood MC at manufacture is 6–8%, and EN tests (EN 314) use 65%RH conditioning.  Manufacturers’ datasheets for hardwood or tropical plywood often list <12% moisture uptake and <2% linear expansion after soaking (both depend on glue type).  

- **MDF:**  European Standard EN 622-5 defines “MDF for humid conditions” (Type H) and “exterior MDF” (Type E), with cyclic humidity and boil tests to qualify water resistance.  Standard MDF (phenol-resin-coated edges aside) is not intended for prolonged damp use; it is tested by EN 317 (24h water soak).  Typical moisture-resistant (MR or HDF) MDF will be marketed with less than ~6–8% thickness swelling after 24h immersion.  Internal bond (EN 319) is measured after wet-cycling for humid-grade MDF【78†L352-L360】【78†L367-L375】.  Standard MDF uses urea-formaldehyde resin (prone to hydrolysis), whereas moisture-resistant MDF often uses melamine-formaldehyde or special additives to reduce hygroscopicity.  

Manufacturers’ technical data often state “≤15% thickness swell (24h water)” for exterior plywood and “≤6%” for MR plywood; for MDF they may quote <8% (MR grade) vs 12–15% (interior grade)【78†L359-L362】.  In absence of local Brunei data, assume tropical plywood (rubberwood, meranti veneers) and imported MDF behave similarly to these specs.  

# Hygro-Mechanical Effects and Irreversibility  
Wood-based panels experience both **elastic swelling** (reversible) and **plastic deformation**.  As RH rises, bound water enters cell walls causing expansion; on drying some shrinkage occurs.  However, repeated or prolonged exposure, and high MC beyond the *fiber saturation point* (~28–30%MC【69†L67-L74】), lead to permanent changes.  For MDF/particleboard, exposure beyond ~80%RH causes collapse of densified fibers and cell structures.  In practice:  

- **Reversible vs Irreversible:**  MDF (and particleboard) undergo **permanent set** after high humidity.  The Composite Panel Association notes that PB/MDF swelling beyond original dimension is *not fully reversible*【69†L95-L100】.  On drying, MDF retains much of its expanded thickness/length.  Plywood is more dimensionally stable due to cross-ply layup and waterproof glue, but under extreme or cyclic wetting it too will accumulate irreversible creep or glue-line creep.  Empirically, plywood soaked and dried multiple times shows gradually increased thickness that does not fully recover.  

- **Fiber Saturation and Glue:**  Once bound water reaches FSP (~28% MC), additional moisture causes free-water condensation.  This does not further swell wood fibers, but **water ingress can pry apart plies**.  Adhesives are critical: phenolic (PF) glues in marine plywood resist this well, whereas urea or phenol–urea (MUF) glues may creep or hydrolyze if continuously wet.  Extended high humidity or boiling tests can break down inferior resins, leading to delamination.  In field terms, plywood labeled “exterior” or “marine” will resist delamination much longer than interior-grade plywood【71†L173-L180】.  

- **Fungal Decay:**  Warm, moist conditions also promote mold/rot.  Wood decay fungi generally require prolonged wood MC above ~20–25%【88†L83-L90】.  In Brunei’s climate (RH often 80–90% so EMC ~12–15%), untreated wood panels can approach these levels and support mold.  MDF, being all-wood/UF, can be especially vulnerable to mold if MC exceeds ~20%.  Marine plywood (with naturally durable woods or preservative glues) resists rot better.  Preventing decay thus demands keeping MC below ~20% (i.e. using sealed panels or controlling humidity).  

# Practical Implications and Mitigation  
Given Brunei’s RH, designers must **minimize moisture uptake** in service.  Strategies include:  

- **Material Selection:** Use moisture-rated products: exterior-grade plywood (MDF with “HMR” or melamine treatment, marine plywood, or chemically treated panelboard).  Plywood clearly outperforms standard MDF in wet conditions【69†L95-L100】【65†L96-L104】; if MDF is used, specify *high-moisture-resistance (HMR)* grades.  For furniture/fitting, choose HDF or exterior phenolic-resin panels.  

- **Surface Treatments:** Seal all panel faces and edges with paint, varnish, or membrane.  Edge sealing is especially critical, as exposed edges wick moisture rapidly【71†L173-L180】.  Waterproof coatings (oil-based primers, polyurethane, epoxy) greatly slow moisture ingress.  

- **Ventilation/Dehumidification:** Maintain indoor RH below ~60% if possible.  Air-conditioning, ceiling fans, or dehumidifiers will reduce sorption.  Ensure good air circulation behind panels or inside cavities.  In new construction, allow panels to **acclimatize** (dry and equilibrate) in occupied humidity before fitting.  

- **Structural Details:** Rely on cross-lamination wherever possible.  Plywood’s alternating grain controls lateral bowing【69†L98-L104】.  When using flat panels, add stiffeners or restraining members (solid lumber frames or trim) to counteract swelling forces【69†L98-L104】. Avoid unbalanced constructions (e.g. veneering MDF without bottom sheet).  

- **Service Conditions:** Keep panels above ground and away from direct rain/splash.  In kitchens/bathrooms, place cabinets on plinths, do not sit cabinetry directly on wet floors.  For shelving, design adjustable clearances to allow swelling space.

# Irreversible Swelling Progression  

```mermaid
flowchart TD
    A[Equilibrated at Ambient RH] --> B[Prolonged High RH Exposure (80-90%)];
    B --> C[Wood Fibers Take Up Bound Water\n(EMC rises towards ~12-15%)];
    C --> D{Fiber Saturation Point Reached?\n(~28-30% MC)};
    D -->|No| E[Elastic Swelling (mostly reversible)];
    D -->|Yes| F[Excess Moisture in Voids and Glue Lines];
    F --> G[Internal Stress / Pressure on Glue Lines];
    G --> H{Material Type: Plywood vs MDF};
    H -->|Plywood| I[Glue-Line Creep or Delamination (esp. inferior glue)];
    H -->|MDF| J[Fiber Collapse and Plastic Set];
    I --> K[Partial Irreversible Dimension Change (some residual swelling)];
    J --> K;
    K --> L[Surface or Structural Failure (warping, cracking, mold)];
```

This flowchart outlines how sustained high humidity drives wood composite panels from a benign equilibrium state toward permanent swelling and damage.  Under cyclic or extreme RH, **fiber saturation** eventually occurs.  In plywood, the weakest link is the glue line (especially in low-grade panels), leading to peel or delamination【71†L173-L180】.  In MDF, the fiber mat itself **collapses** irreversibly (densified fibers re-expand and do not fully compress back)【69†L95-L100】.  Once irreversible set accumulates, dimensional changes and strength loss become permanent.  

# Assumptions & Data Gaps  

- **Environmental Conditions:** We assume indoor panels see the same humidity as outdoor Brunei (80–90% RH) unless dehumidification is used. We do not have measured indoor RH data for specific Brunei buildings; in practice, air-conditioned interiors might be lower.  
- **Material Grades:** Specific plywood and MDF grades in Brunei are not documented here. We assume use of common tropical/structural plywood and generic MDF. If local preferences (e.g. kempas vs meranti, Chinese vs European MDF) differ, properties may vary.  
- **Numeric Data:** Many values (EMC%, swell%) are drawn from general wood science rather than Brunei‑specific tests. Actual plywood/MDF data sheets or ASTM/EN test results for the exact products are needed for precision.  
- **Creep and Cycles:** Quantification of “number of cycles to failure” is beyond scope; we note qualitatively that repeated high-humidity cycles cause accumulating irreversible creep.  
- **Coating Effectiveness:** The effectiveness of coatings (paint, veneer) in Brunei conditions is assumed similar to temperate climates; tropical UV and rainfall may degrade sealants faster.  

**Data Gaps:**  We could not find Brunei‑specific moisture or durability standards.  Local exposure tests or long-term field studies would fill gaps (e.g. measuring moisture uptake in panels placed in Brunei humidity over months).  Manufacturer data for regionally available HMR panels would allow more accurate dimensional-change forecasts.  

**Sources:**  Key insights were drawn from wood science handbooks (sorption isotherms【60†L113-L121】), wood-panel engineering texts【65†L96-L104】【78†L352-L360】, industry bulletins【69†L95-L100】【88†L83-L90】, and regional climate data【91†L153-L162】.
